## The Lie

"Flip the DNS record and traffic moves to the backup." Every engineer has said this. Almost none of them have measured how long the flip actually takes. DNS is a globally distributed cache with no eviction button — you can lower the TTL, but you can't flush the resolvers that already have your record.

## TTL Reality

A 300s TTL means "caches may keep this for 5 minutes." It does not mean "traffic moves in 5 minutes." Recursive resolvers, ISP caches, and the JVM's own cache all hold the old record until _their_ timer expires — and some ignore the TTL entirely. Real failover via DNS is measured in hours, not seconds.

## What to Use Instead

For fast failover, use something that routes per-request, not per-cache:

- A load balancer with health checks (ALB, HAProxy) — traffic moves the instant the target fails its health check.
- A service mesh (Envoy, Linkerd) — the sideway re-routes without touching DNS.
- An anycast IP (Cloudflare, AWS Global Accelerator) — the network itself routes around failure.

## When DNS Is Fine

DNS failover is fine for _planned_ migrations with a long overlap window, or for low-stakes traffic where a few hours of stale records don't matter. It is not fine for "the primary is down, send traffic to the backup now."

## Conclusion

DNS is a directory, not a router. Use it to _find_ your service; use something else to _move_ to it. The day you treat DNS as a failover switch is the day your outage lasts exactly as long as the longest TTL in the wild.
