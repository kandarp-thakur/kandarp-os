## The Problem

Rolling updates replace pods one batch at a time, but a naive rollout can still take your service down. If readiness probes are misconfigured, traffic routes to containers that aren't ready. If termination is abrupt, in-flight requests die mid-flight. The fleet survives only when every step of the update is coordinated.

## Rolling Updates

A `Deployment` in Kubernetes describes a desired state. When you change the pod template, the controller reconciles toward it — spinning up new pods and draining old ones in waves. The two knobs that govern the wave size are `maxSurge` and `maxUnavailable`.

```bash
$ kubectl set image deployment/api api=api:v2
deployment.apps/api image updated
$ kubectl rollout status deployment/api
deployment "api" successfully rolled out
```

### maxSurge

`maxSurge` is how many _extra_ pods you allow above the desired count during the rollout. A higher surge speeds up the rollout by creating new pods in parallel, at the cost of more replicas (and more resources) running temporarily.

### maxUnavailable

`maxUnavailable` is how many pods you allow to be _below_ the desired count during the rollout. Setting it to `0` guarantees you never drop below your desired capacity — the safest option, at the cost of a slower rollout that needs spare capacity to surge into.

## Health Checks

Readiness probes gate traffic. A pod only receives requests once its readiness probe succeeds, and stops receiving them the moment it fails. This is what makes a rolling update safe: new pods don't take traffic until they're actually ready.

```yaml
readinessProbe:
    httpGet:
        path: /healthz
        port: 8080
    initialDelaySeconds: 5
    periodSeconds: 5
```

Liveness probes are different — they restart a stuck container, they don't gate traffic. Confusing the two is the most common cause of "mysterious" downtime during rollouts.

## Surge & Drain

The safe sequence is: surge new pods → wait for readiness → drain old pods → wait for termination grace. Drain means removing the pod from the Service endpoints _before_ sending it `SIGTERM`, so no new traffic arrives while it shuts down.

## Pitfalls

- **Long shutdowns without `terminationGracePeriodSeconds`** — Kubernetes sends `SIGTERM`, waits 30s, then `SIGKILL`. If your app needs longer to drain, raise the grace period.
- **Readiness probe that never fails** — a probe that always returns 200 lets broken pods keep receiving traffic.
- **Ignoring `preStop` hooks** — a `preStop` sleep gives load balancers time to de-register before `SIGTERM`.

## Conclusion

Zero-downtime isn't magic — it's the disciplined combination of surge, readiness, graceful shutdown, and patience. Treat every rollout as a controlled fire: small, observable, and reversible.
