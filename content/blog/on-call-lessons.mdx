## The First Page

The first time you get paged at 3am, you panic. You don't know what the alert means, you don't know where the runbook is, and the dashboard you need is on a screen you can't find. You fix it by accident, write a bad postmortem, and go back to sleep. Everyone does.

## Alert Fatigue

By page fifty, you've stopped reading the alert text. You see the name, you know the fix, you click the button. This is alert fatigue — and it's dangerous, because the one time the alert means something _new_, you'll miss it. The cure is ruthless alert pruning: every alert that fired without action gets deleted.

> ▸ "An alert that doesn't require action is noise. Delete it."
> — SRE proverb

## Runbooks That Work

A good runbook answers three questions in the first three lines: what's broken, how to check, how to fix. Everything else is reference. The runbooks I actually used at 3am were the short ones — bullet points, copy-paste commands, a link to the dashboard.

## The Human Factor

A year of pages teaches you that incidents are mostly human, not technical. The deploy that broke prod was unreviewed. The alert that woke you was un-tuned. The rollback that didn't work was never tested. You can't fix these with another dashboard — you fix them with process and sleep.

## What I'd Change

If I started the year over, I'd spend the first month not on alerts but on _removing_ them. Half the pages I got were noise; fixing that would have given me back a month of sleep. The second thing: every incident gets a runbook, even a one-liner, before the next on-call shift starts.
